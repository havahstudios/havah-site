# Voice & Style Guide — HAVAH Blog Posts

Rules for how to write blog posts: sentence length, pacing, formality, and paragraph structure. Based on studying the writing style of a design agency whose tone works well for small business audiences. Rules only — no copied phrasing.

---

## Formality Level: Conversational Professional

Write like you're talking to a smart business owner who doesn't have time to waste.

- Use contractions always: "it's," "you've," "don't," "we're" — never "it is" or "do not" unless you're making a deliberate point
- Use second person throughout: "you," "your website," "your brand" — not "businesses" or "clients"
- Avoid corporate filler: "leverage," "utilize," "robust," "seamless" — say what you mean in plain words
- No hedging: state things as facts, not possibilities. "Good design builds trust" not "good design may help build trust"
- Occasional plain-talk signals are fine: "In short:", "Here's the thing:", "Spoiler:" — used once or twice per post, not every section

---

## Sentence Length: Mix Short With Long

Long sentences explain. Short sentences land the point.

A pattern that works well:
1. Medium sentence that sets up an idea
2. Longer sentence that explains or gives context
3. Short sentence that closes it hard

Example of the rhythm (not actual content to copy):
> "Your website is the first thing most customers see. That means every font, every colour, every second it takes to load is doing either damage or good. No neutrals."

Rules:
- Never write three long sentences in a row — the reader checks out
- Never write three short sentences in a row — it starts to feel choppy and thin
- One-sentence paragraphs work, but use them sparingly. They carry weight only when most sentences are longer
- Fragments are fine for emphasis: "That decision? Already made."

---

## Paragraph Structure: Lead With the Point

Don't warm up before making the point. Say it first.

**Structure of a strong paragraph:**
1. **Opening line**: the main claim or fact — not a warm-up
2. **Middle**: one or two sentences of explanation, context, or example
3. **Closing line**: consequence, implication, or a short punch

**Max length**: 3–4 sentences. If a paragraph is getting longer, find the split.

**Start paragraphs with strong words**, not weak ones:
- Strong: "Design affects how people feel."
- Weak: "It is important to note that design affects..."
- Strong: "Fast websites rank better."
- Weak: "One thing to be aware of is that..."

---

## Pacing: Keep It Moving

Readers scan before they commit. The writing should reward both.

- **Short paragraphs** — 2–4 sentences. Give the eye somewhere to rest
- **Subheadings** are guideposts, not decoration. Write them like complete thoughts or questions the reader is asking, not just topic labels
- After a dense explanation, follow with something short. Give the reader a breath
- Don't linger. Make the point, give it room, move on
- Lists are useful when you have three or more comparable items — don't make a list of two things, just write a sentence

---

## Analogies: Concrete and Specific

When using an analogy, make it tangible — not abstract or corporate.

- Good: bicycles vs Teslas, meal prepping, renting vs building a house
- Not good: "like a symphony," "like a puzzle," "like a journey"

The best analogies:
- Reference something the reader knows from daily life
- Land in one or two sentences — don't over-explain them
- Sometimes extend one step further: not just "like renting" but "like renting an apartment — you're in someone else's place, limited by their rules"

Don't force an analogy into every section. One or two per post is enough.

---

## Transitions: Minimal and Direct

Don't write long transitions between ideas. Pivot with a connector or just start the new idea.

Works:
- "Here's the problem:"
- "That changes things."
- "The fix isn't complicated."
- A colon to introduce the next thought directly

Avoid:
- "Furthermore," "Additionally," "Moreover," "In conclusion,"
- "As we mentioned earlier," "Building on the above,"
- Any phrase that could be deleted without losing meaning

---

## Opening a Post: Hook With a Consequence

Don't ease in. Start with something the reader cares about — a cost, a missed opportunity, a decision already being made.

Strong openings lead with what's at stake before naming the topic. The reader should understand why this matters before they understand what it's about.

Then name the problem clearly and signal you're going to solve it.

---

## Closing a Section or Post: Forward-Looking, Short

Don't summarise what you just said — the reader just read it.

Close with:
- What comes next
- What the reader should do or think about
- A short punchy line that leaves them with something

The last sentence of a section should feel like a door opening, not a door closing.

---

## What to Avoid

- **Passive voice**: "a decision is made" → "you decide"
- **Redundant qualifiers**: "very," "really," "quite," "extremely" — cut them
- **Noun stacks**: "website design cost optimisation strategy" — break these up
- **Starting sentences with "There is" or "It is"**: find the real subject
- **Long run-on sentences with multiple commas**: split them
- **Exclamation marks**: resist. If a sentence needs one to land, rewrite it

---

## Quick Self-Check Before Publishing

Read the draft aloud. If you stumble, the sentence is too long or awkwardly structured.  
Ask yourself about each paragraph: *what's the point of this?* If the answer takes more than one sentence, the paragraph is doing too much.
