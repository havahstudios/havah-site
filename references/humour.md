# Humour Style Guide — HAVAH Blog Posts

Rules for how jokes and personality show up in writing. Based on the writing sample provided.

---

## The Core Principle: Be Yourself

The only humour that works is humour that feels genuine. Readers can immediately tell when something is performed vs. natural. The voice in the writing sample is self-aware, slightly self-deprecating, and comfortable making references that not everyone will get — because that authenticity is what makes it land.

If a joke feels forced while writing it, it will feel forced while reading it. Cut it.

---

## How Often to Use It

**Sprinkle, don't drench.**

Humour isn't like hot sauce — you can't put it on everything. One or two moments per post is enough. When used sparingly, a funny line becomes a memorable moment. When used constantly, it becomes exhausting and readers stop trusting that the content is actually useful.

- One joke per 400–500 words is a reasonable ceiling
- Strong sections (stats, key arguments, FAQs) don't need humour — let them do their job
- The intro and the closing are the best spots to let personality show

---

## What Works

**Self-aware asides.** Acknowledging the absurdity of what you just wrote, in the moment.
> *"[Switches to serious face]"*
> *"(that hurt's to say)"*

This style works because it invites the reader in rather than performing at them.

**Analogies with a twist.** The dart-throwing comparison in the sample works because it's relatable and slightly unexpected — not the obvious metaphor.

**Callbacks.** Setting up something early and returning to it later (Nickelback gets mentioned twice). It rewards readers who are paying attention.

**Pop culture references used lightly.** Jay-Z, Jack Black, Robin Williams — references that are broad enough to land across audiences without being so current they'll age badly in six months.

**Parenthetical honesty.** A quick aside in brackets or parentheses can be funnier than a full sentence because it mimics how people actually think.

---

## What Doesn't Work

- **Cheesy.** Nothing that sounds like it belongs on a motivational poster.
- **Dad jokes** — unless the setup is so perfect that it genuinely lands. Doubt = cut it.
- **Forced humour every paragraph.** It makes the writing feel like it's trying too hard and undermines the actual content.
- **Controversial or charged topics.** The goal is to connect, not to lose half the audience over a joke.
- **Hyper-specific references** that only a small group will understand. General > niche, especially while the audience is still growing.
- **Anything above PG-13.** Keep it professional enough that a client could read it without cringing.

---

## Don't Be Afraid to Miss

Even the best comedians have jokes that bomb. If something doesn't land, cut it in the next post — don't abandon the personality entirely. Humour in writing improves with practice, and occasional misses are part of finding what works.

---

## One-Line Test

Before keeping a joke, ask: *does this make the writing better, or is it just there to be funny?*

If it's only there to be funny, cut it. If it makes the point land harder or makes the reader feel like they're in good hands — keep it.
